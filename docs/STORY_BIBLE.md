# BRAM: The Almost Boy — Story Bible

This is the canonical reference for character, world, and theme.
Future code, copy, and asset decisions must not contradict it. If a
new beat conflicts with the bible, update the bible *first*, then the
code.

## Premise

Bram is a boy who lives in a house by the sea. One night a blue-white
flash and an electrical orb over the water draw him downstairs. In
the kitchen he meets Nilo — an interdimensional being of pure energy
who is trying, and failing, to repair a broken world he cannot
touch. Bram offers help. He gives too much, too quickly. He becomes
a skeleton. Nilo, holding Bram's offered potential, gains a human
form. Together — with an Owl who finds them — they begin to fix the
world, one careful thing at a time.

## Characters

### Bram
- A human boy. Curious. Brave when dressed. Gentle.
- Becomes a skeleton because he gives Life Sparks to the world, not
  because he is attacked or stolen from.
- After the prologue he is a walking skeleton with a skateboard.
  Cozy, magical, funny — never horror.
- Plays the mentor / older-sibling / father-figure role to Nilo
  about how the physical world works.
- Core lesson: **"Just enough."**

### Nilo
- An interdimensional being of energy, electricity, and potential.
- **Cannot touch the physical world directly.** Needs Bram as a
  bridge.
- Appears first as a blue electrical orb over the water; in the
  kitchen as a translucent blue alien apparition with flowing
  tendrils, inner star-lights, and large glowing eyes.
- Takes a distinct **human form** after Bram gives himself: dark
  blue-black swept hair, pale cool-tinted skin, teal tunic with
  golden belt, soft blue undertone glow. Visually different from
  Bram so the two are never confused.
- **Not a villain. Did not steal Bram's body.** Nilo is a partner
  who arrived without form and was given one by Bram's kindness.
- Core question: **"What's next?"**

### Owl
- Arrives at the kitchen window after the transformation.
- A gentle frame-setter; names what happened without judgement:
  *"He gave too much, and you had nowhere to put it."*
- Guides Bram through the chapter restoration arcs in the slice
  (hands → feet → eyes → voice → heart → human).

## Setting

- **The real world**: Bram's coastal house, the bedroom, hallway,
  kitchen, ocean outside.
- **The Almost World**: a sibling reality that bleeds into the real
  world wherever something is broken — bridges, clocktowers,
  gardens, markets, looping paths. The game chapters take place
  there.

## Transformation Rules

- Bram becomes a skeleton because he **gave too much, too quickly**.
- Each "Life Spark" is a piece of his humanity offered to the world:
  hands, feet, eyes, voice, heart. Restoring them is the
  game's progression spine.
- Nilo becomes physical because Bram's offered potential filled the
  empty shape he was. He did not take it; he was given it.
- Restoration through play returns Bram's pieces to him over time.

## Themes

- **Generosity vs. measure.** Giving is good. Giving without measure
  scatters you. The game teaches the *measure*, not the *withholding*.
- **Fixing is sequential.** You cannot fix everything at once.
- **Math is careful repair.** Amount, timing, fairness, pattern,
  sequence, balance. Each game mode trains one facet of "careful."

## Mantras (use verbatim where possible)

- Game mantra: **"Fix one thing at a time."**
- Bram's lesson: **"Just enough."**
- Nilo's question: **"What's next?"**

## Tone Guardrails

- Cozy storybook fantasy, warm lantern light, parchment UI, soft
  parallax.
- Mistakes are funny and gentle (Bram falls apart in pieces, then
  pieces itself back). Never frightening, never punishing.
- Nilo speaks in short, slightly off-kilter sentences as he learns
  the world ("Knees are unstable." "I have edges.").
- Owl speaks plainly and warmly.

## Do-Nots

- Do not write Nilo as a thief, ghost, demon, or trickster.
- Do not write Bram as a victim. He chose to help.
- Do not show the skeleton transformation as scary or violent.
- Do not let any character resolve the broken world in one stroke.
  The mantra forbids it.
- Do not give Nilo and Bram visually similar designs — they must
  read as two distinct people the moment you see them on screen.
